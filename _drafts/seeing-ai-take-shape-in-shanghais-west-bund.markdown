 This week, we take a look at **Shanghai’s AI Tower** in the West Bund innovation cluster, part of our China Programme. The visit explores how artificial intelligence, MedTech, robotics and immersive technologies are being developed inside one of Shanghai’s most ambitious innovation districts.

#### From waterfront regeneration to AI cluster

The West Bund sits on the Huangpu riverside in Shanghai’s Xuhui District. It is already known for culture, design and urban regeneration, but it is also becoming one of the city’s major technology districts.

At its centre is the [West Bund AI Valley](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-SpecialIndustrialParks/20240202/2e3c00d502ef4845b61a75c2b8ab3ccb.html), a 32-hectare district with about 1.2 million square metres of construction area. The area is anchored by the West Bund International AI Tower and is designed to bring together AI companies, start-ups, research institutions and supply chain businesses.

The wider area is also connected to some of China’s largest technology companies. West Bund material highlights Alibaba within the AI Valley, while Shanghai reporting has also linked the district with companies such as Tencent, Huawei, Microsoft and SenseTime. This is important because the visit is not only about one building. It is about seeing how a whole ecosystem is being organised around technology, talent, research and commercial application.

#### Starting from the 45th floor

The visit begins on the 45th floor of the AI Tower, with an introduction to the area’s regeneration programme and the companies based in and around the cluster.

This gives students a clear starting point. From above, the West Bund can be understood as more than a waterfront development. It is a planned innovation district, where property, public policy, universities, investors and technology companies are being brought together.

The view helps explain why place still matters in a digital economy. AI may be built with code, data and computing power, but it also depends on physical infrastructure, business networks, talent pipelines and places where companies can meet, test and grow.

#### The Foundation Model Innovation Center

A key part of the cluster is the [Shanghai Foundation Model Innovation Center](https://english.shanghai.gov.cn/en-Latest-WhatsNew/20250430/d641c50ef4f94163947dfbe27072c15e.html). Foundation models are the large AI systems behind many newer tools and applications, supporting areas such as language, images, audio, coding, research and customer service.

The centre began with 21 foundation-model enterprises. Within 100 days, that number had passed 50. It is now home to more than 100 enterprises and has helped create a wider ecosystem of nearly 400 related companies in Xuhui. It has also supported 43 registered foundation models, around 61% of Shanghai’s total.

These figures show the speed of development, but the more interesting point is how the centre works. It is not only offering office space. It supports companies with computing power, data, finance, talent and application matchmaking. In plain terms, it helps young companies move from research and early ideas towards products, customers and scale.

#### HKUST and the university-start-up link

The visit also includes a tour of the [Hong Kong University of Science and Technology Shanghai Center](https://hkust.edu.hk/news/opening-hkust-shanghai-center-xuhui-district), based in the AI Tower.

This part of the programme looks at how HKUST acts as an incubator for AI, MedTech and other cutting-edge start-ups. It also shows how academic research, young talent and technology companies can work together inside the same ecosystem.

This is a useful way to understand the role universities can play beyond teaching. In sectors such as AI, robotics, biomedicine and deep technology, start-ups often need access to research expertise, technical facilities, mentors, investors and industry partners. The university becomes part of the innovation process, not separate from it.

#### Founder-led technology demonstrations

The visit includes presentations from start-up founders demonstrating the latest technologies, from robotics and virtual reality to AI applications.

This is where the ecosystem becomes practical. Students are not only hearing about innovation in general terms. They are meeting founders, seeing products and asking how new technologies are developed, tested and brought to market.

That gives the group a deeper understanding of current technology trends and the environment around them. They can see how start-ups fit into a wider network of universities, major tech companies, investors and city-level planning.
